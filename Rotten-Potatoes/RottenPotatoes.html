Rotten Potatoes: Learning on the Yelp Dataset Challenge

Abstract

The objective of this report is to characterize the norms of expensive restaurants. By a combination of dining at expensive restaurants and observing their representation in media, many of us have some preconceived notion of the experience we expect to have at an expensive restaurant, certain features that come along with a hefty bill at the end of our meal. Perhaps we imagine having to book reservations a month in advance; a wine list with more options than there are entrees; diners dressed in suits and gowns; refined, finessed European food. Whatever our individual notions of an expensive restaurant may be, they are undoubtedly shaped by a likely small sample size of personal experiences and a bias from television and movies. Rather than trust our instincts, this project puts the experience of an expensive restaurant against hard data. We conducted machine learning and data analysis on the Yelp Dataset Challenge data – a collection of 25000 restaurants across 10 cities, including over 1600 expensive restaurants (those classified as $$$ or $$$$) – to determine with more authority what norms expensive restaurants tend to follow.

Data

At the outset of our project, we sought to mine our own data so that we would be able to choose which cities we worked with. We began by scraping multiple review websites for information about restaurants in Providence. Although the scraping was successful, the initial analysis of our data quickly revealed it to be far too limited in scope. For example, one of our analyses split our data into 20 categories of restaurant genre, such as “Cafes”, “Asian”, “European”, etc. With 20 distinct categories, we saw that having 200 restaurants was completely insufficient; we would need to scrape something more like 2000 to begin deriving real results.

After this initial setback, we instead decided to use the data from the Yelp Dataset Challenge: a bank of over 77000 businesses, including over 25000 restaurants. Each business is represented as a JSON object, and we extracted what we felt to be the relevant fields (namely, its attributes and rating) in order to conduct our analysis. In addition to immediately solving our problem with scope, the Yelp dataset provides the additional benefit of having a long list of attributes for each business, such as whether or not it serves alcohol, the expected diner attire, catering and carryout options, and many more. These attributes are completely exclusive to the Yelp Dataset Challenge; they aren’t even made available through the Yelp API. With our goal of using restaurant features to characterize expensive restaurants, these features were an immense benefit that we couldn’t find anywhere else.

The dataset required some normalization and cleaning. The 69 food categories were condensed into 20 more general areas, and any labels with a non-binary value set were converted into binary terms. For example, the “Attire” label had three possible values: casual, dressy, and formal. We grouped these into two classifications by treating “dressy” and “formal” as equivalent values. In addition, each restaurant JSON object had only a limited subset of all possible labels, meaning that further exploration of the data was needed to determine how to treat missing values.

Hypothesis

Although we ended up only analyzing data from Yelp, we initially intended to compare different review sites across restaurants and measure them for differences. We hypothesized that we would find significant differences in average review scores between Yelp and TripAdvisor, for example, and that these differences would indicate a difference in the user demographic of these two websites. In the Yelp/TripAdvisor example, we thought that reviews on TripAdvisor might be higher because TripAdvisor is used more by tourists, who would tend to derive a better restaurant experience just by being on vacation. As discussed in the Data section, we ran into trouble when our data was insufficiently sized, especially when we began only including restaurants that existed on both websites, further reducing our dataset.

After we took on the Yelp Dataset, our hypothesis changed to better utilize the information provided by Yelp. Broadly, our goal was to take some feature and then measure its correlation against other features. We found a strong choice in characterizing the norms of price, since every restaurant has a price level and since price is a central factor of every dining experience. Our hypothesis became that the price of a restaurant can be classified based on its other features; namely, that an expensive restaurant is good for dinner, takes reservations, is considered upscale and classy, serves alcohol, has dressy or formal attire, has waiter service, does not offer take-out, delivery, or catering services, and is quiet or average in terms of noise level. More succinctly, our objective in this report is to show that an expensive restaurant is about more than high-quality ingredients or advanced cooking techniques; rather, the bill we pay at the end of a meal can be determined by the services we received before and during our dining experience.

Methodology

We approached our hypothesis by analyzing the Yelp data via a variety of machine learning algorithms, as well as through interactive visualizations of both the raw data and of the results produced by machine learning.

Note: All visualizations can be viewed by running a local server (e.g. Python’s http.server) in our project’s Github directory and accessing the .html files in the visualizations folder. For convenient viewing, screenshots are included in this report; but most of the visualizations are interactive, so loading them locally will provide the best experience.

Scatterplot Matrix

To provide an initial gateway to our results, we generated a scatterplot matrix across the dimensions identified as most important for our decision tree – that is, the price level of restaurants plotted against reservations, take-out, and attire features. Unlike the decision tree and regression, which are described below, the scatterplot matrix is not undersampled, so it shows all 25000+ restaurants (each dot on the matrix represents 100 restaurants, for clarity). Conclusions are not meant to be explicitly drawn from the scatterplot; rather, they represent another way of looking at the data further explored and broken down by our decision tree, as well as a general idea of the shape of the Yelp Dataset.

Bar Charts

To give the user an easy way to visualize patterns and trends in context of price tiers and food categories through the lens of restaurant ratings, we created an interactive bar chart. The chart has four modes: price tier vs. average rating, food category vs. average rating, and two combination graphs (price tier and food category vs. average rating). The user can filter out unnecessary information through the checkboxes. To accommodate the many different combinations of data possible, and the ability to add more dimensions of data in the future, each bar is calculated dynamically as the user selects checkboxes. This comes with the disadvantage of some hangtime between checking a box and generating the chart, but we believe its advantages are significant.

Decision Tree

We used Python’s sklearn to generate decision trees to classify restaurants as either “cheap” or “expensive”, based on the thirteen attributes that have the least missing data for the restaurants in our dataset (‘stars’, ‘takeout’, ‘drivethru’, ‘outdoorseating’, ‘caters’, ‘noise’, ‘delivery’, ‘attire’, ‘gf_dinner’, ‘reservations’, ‘a_classy’, ‘waiter’, ‘alcohol’).

A challenge we faced in working with the data is that only 1666 (~7%) of the restaurants in our dataset are classified as expensive ($$$ or $$$$), so we had to perform undersampling in order to accuracy train our decision trees. Undersampling simply consisted of randomly sampling 1666 cheap restaurants, combining these with the 1666 expensive restaurants, and running a 10-fold cross-validation to obtain a mean accuracy score. This process is repeated multiple times, each with a different random sample of cheap restaurants. Overall, we achieved an accuracy of ~81%, which is a satisfyingly high accuracy given that our dataset contains lots of missing data (we decided to set all “n/a” values to “False”). We briefly experimented with random forests to see if that would improve our accuracy, but it only made an incremental improvement of less than one percent. We ultimately determined that our dataset is simply missing too much data to improve beyond an accuracy of ~81% using decision trees.

SVM

We experimented with using sklearn’s SVC (a Radial Basis Function SVM) to perform the same task of classifying restaurants as cheap or expensive. Tuning the parameters of our SVM to maximize the accuracy was a little tricky, but we approached it by training and testing various combinations of the parameters C and gamma (a highly computationally expensive task that was only performed once before they were hard-coded into the code). In the end, we found that keeping gamma at the default value of ‘auto’ yielded the best results, while setting C to approximately 0.10 greatly improved the accuracy over the default value of 1.0. This makes sense, because a low C value makes the decision surface smooth, which prevents overfitting. With our tuned SVC, we were able to achieve an accuracy of 82.5% using the same method of undersampling we used for the decision trees. This is not a dramatic improvement over our decision tree/random forest classifiers, but our SVM does appear to have a superior accuracy, at the cost of being more computationally expensive.

Regression

When formulating our regression, our dependent variable was the price of the restaurant (1 for $$$ and $$$$ and 0 for $ and $$ restaurants) and used numerous independent variables. When selecting for independent variables, we selected the same dependent variables that were used in the decision tree.

Additionally, we also wanted to incorporate restaurant category into our regression variables, so we condensed the original 50+ restaurant categories into 20 restaurant categories: Bars, Chinese, European, Dessert, Korean, Cafes, Mediterranean, Japanese, French, Food Trucks, Non-American, American, Non-White, Sandwiches, Latin America, Pizza, Thai, Asian, Brunch, Italian. These new categories were not distinct, so restaurants could have multiple categories. These 20 categories were then set up as dummy variables, which could then be regressed upon.

The file regression/restaurants_all_with_price.csv was used as the cleaned data set for the regression. The actual Python regression was performed in the file regression/regression.py, but there was more nuanced analysis in Stata of which we copied some output here. The files category_creator.py, dataset_creator.py, and add_cheap_expensive_to_csvs.py were used to process the data from the file /data/attributes_all.txt.

We naively set up a logistic regression using LogisticRegression from the package sklearn.linear_model. Price was set as labels and all other attributes were used as features. We also undersampled from our cheap restaurants, so that we had 1666 expensive restaurants and 1666 cheap restaurants. Running a basic logistic regression then yielded a surprisingly high level of ~84% accuracy when running multiple iterations and taking a random sample of 1666 restaurants from the ~18,000 total cheap restaurants every single time.

However, we wanted to further refine our regression and understand statistical significance, so we moved away from the blackbox of sklearn to Stata. We first attempted to fit our data with the linear probability model, which is OLS regression with a binary dependent variable. This initial regression yielded the following regression tables:

From this, some interesting trends already begin to emerge. We see that the majority of category dummy variables do not add any significant meaning to our regression because we fail to reject the null hypothesis that these variables are 0 at a 95% significance level. The variables that appear to not have statistically significant effects are european, korean, brunch, thai, latin american, asian, nonwhite, american, nonamerican, french, japanese, mediterranean, cafes, italian, dessert, sandwiches, gf_dinner, noise, and outdoorseating. Some variables had strong positive correlations, so they were more indicative of expensive restaurants and they include: reservations, attire, and classy. Some variables that have strong negative correlations and were indicative of cheap restaurants are takeout, drivethru, catering, chinese, and pizza.

This then allowed us to set up a regression with only significant variables and is displayed above. However, the linear probability model is not perfect in that probabilities can exceed 1 or dip below 0, which makes no sense as probabilities must be bounded by 0 and 1.

Therefore, we once again returned to logistic regression through the logit model in stata. Running a logit regression

          . logit expensive stars takeout drivethru caters delivery attire reservations classy waiter bars chinese cafes pizza

with the variables that we determined were significant in the earlier regression gives us a model of statistically significant independent variables that can help us classify restaurants based on price. Ultimately, the regression could be further refined by applying more rigorous econometric analysis and thinking more about interactions between variable and perhaps looking at whether omitted variable bias or simultaneous causality may be affecting our regression in any sense.

Results

Given the accuracy of our undersampled models, we can conclude success in classifying and predicting a restaurant’s price based on common features like reservations, take-out, attire, and genre. This provides some slightly provocative insight into restaurant menu pricing: our results suggest that the price of a restaurant can be determined based on the existence of features that have nothing at all to do with food. Taking reservations, denying takeout, expecting formal attire, and other norms, we conclude, are strong determinants of an expensive restaurant. Of course, this does not imply that expensive restaurants don’t have good food; further studies would be needed to confirm or deny that claim. However, we can suggest that, generally speaking, even if a restaurant has superior quality food, its prices will be lower unless it provides the services listed above.

As stated in the Data and Hypothesis sections, we were unable to achieve our original goals of comparing rating trends between websites, due to unexpected troubles we encountered concerning limited data-scraping ability and difficulty in collecting a significantly large amount of data. However, in our new hypothesis, we were pleasantly surprised to find a very successful outcome in breaking down and analyzing the Yelp Dataset.

Further Questions

Exploration of the Yelp dataset brought up many new areas of interest, but ones that were not necessarily feasible given that specific set of data and the focus of our project. One of the areas we are most interested in exploring is analyzing cultural culinary differences by geographical location. For example, Chicago is famous for its pizza while New York City is known to have good Chinese food; our interest lies in applying statistical methods to restaurant reviews corresponding to these categories, areas, and locations to detect whether or not there are any significant differences. Given that the cities in the Yelp dataset were not well-known for any particular area, we would have to scrape Yelp for the data needed for this analysis.

Another possible direction of exploration would be trying to compare the “quality” of food served at a restaurant to its features, to see if high-price restaurants really do have better food that would justify a higher price. Conversely, does adding features like reservations and waiter service increase the perceived quality of food? How much of a role does the dining experience play in our perception of flavor? This question would add more weight to the conclusion found in this paper; unfortunately, many of these metrics would be very difficult to quantify, and would certainly contain a large amount of subjectivity.
